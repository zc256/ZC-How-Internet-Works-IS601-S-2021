Routing


Definition

  • How a packet gets from origin to destination
  • The packets do not need to follow a fixed path, as it is similar to how a driver uses GPS to find the optimal route
  • Many kinds of digital information can be sent with IP packets, but there are limits
    • Information must be sent in different packets
    • These packets may take different paths and arrive at the destination at different times, but the original information will be received all the same
    • The packets do not choose their route, they only have the information of where it came from, and where it is going
    • Routers act like traffic managers, directing packets to where they need to go, and trying to create as little traffic as possible. Packets may then arrive at different times, or possibly out of order.
  • Internet Protocol
    • Every router keeps track of multiple paths for sending packets, and chooses the cheapest path for sending the packets
    • Often the best route for data to travel is not the most direct
    • More options = fault tolerant/reliable
  • Transmission Control Protocol (TCP)
    • Manages the sending and receiving of all your data as packets
    • TCP takes all packets and does an inventory check to make sure all packets have been received. The packets if they are missing, will then be resent

Examples


Why do we use Routing?

  • Routers act as traffic managers, and allow for packets to traverse the network in the most optimal path
  • Without routing, there is a good chance packets may never arrive to their destination, or might arrive out of order. Either way, the original message sent will never be available or easily read by its destination
